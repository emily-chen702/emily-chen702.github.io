Abstract: Alzheimer’s disease is the most prevalent form of dementia, affecting approximately 5.7 million individuals. It is characterized by irreversible, progressive brain damage that destroys cognitive functions ranging from memory and reasoning to comprehension. When afflicted with Alzheimer’s, the brain becomes filled with abnormal beta-amyloid protein deposits that inhibit and kill neuronal cells essential for cognitive ability. These proteins suffocate neurons, destroying not only the neuron but also to the neurological connections associated with this neuron.

Unfortunately, the scientific knowledge of this disease is extremely limited, leaving a fatal neurological disorder lacking an effective treatment. Inspired by proton beam therapy used for cancer, we believe that particle accelerators can allow for the denaturation of beta-amyloid proteins that cause neurological deterioration in Alzheimer’s.

When: July 2015 – August 2015

Abstract: Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR)/Cas9 is a DNA editing tool currently being used, that can modify the genetic sequence of organisms. Because of specificity, this newfound technology has the potential to lessen the effects of genetic diseases, such as Parkinson’s. Parkinson’s is a neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the damage or destruction of key dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra. Although Parkinson’s is not typically inherited, certain genetic markers have been shown to increase the chance of Parkinson’s. In humans, the G2019S mutation on the LRRK2 gene is one of the most common genetic markers for Parkinson’s. This gene is homologous to the LRK-1 gene in Caenorhabditis elegans (C. elegans). Due to genetic similarities between C. elegans and humans, we can find the aforementioned mutation and attempt to correct it. We believe that by targeting the specific mutations in C. elegans with the CRISPR/Cas9 technology, we may weaken or eliminate the disease entirely.
